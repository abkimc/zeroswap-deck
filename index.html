BLOCKCHAIN CONTEXT

Why does this problem live on a blockchain?

Blockchains let anyone trade without trusting a central party — but that trustlessness comes with a price: latency.

🏦

Centralised Exchange (Binance)

A company runs servers, holds your funds, and updates prices in milliseconds. Very fast and liquid — but you trust them.

⛓️

Decentralised Exchange (Uniswap)

A smart contract on Ethereum holds funds and runs a pricing formula. No central party — but updates arrive through blockchain transactions and blocks, not millisecond exchange servers. Ethereum blocks are roughly 12 seconds apart.

⏱️ The block-time window of danger

While the external market moves, the pool can still reflect its last on-chain price until a new transaction updates it. “Yesterday's price” was only a metaphor and was too strong: the real issue is a short-lived but exploitable price delay.

ZeroSwap's solution must live entirely on-chain — no external price feeds, no secret computations, and cheap enough in gas to be practical.

⚠️
THE PROBLEM

Why do liquidity providers lose money?

Liquidity Providers (LPs) deposit tokens into the pool so traders can swap. They earn fees — but face a structural adversary: arbitrageurs.

What is an AMM?

AMM means Automated Market Maker: a smart contract that lets users trade against a pool of tokens instead of matching them with a human order book.

🦈

Step 1 — Price moves on Binance

ETH jumps from $3,000 → $3,050 on Binance. Uniswap still prices it at $3,000. A $50 gap opens.

💸

Step 2 — Arbitrageur attacks

Bot buys ETH cheap from Uniswap at $3,000, sells on Binance at $3,050. $50 profit per ETH — entirely funded by the LP pool.

Loss-Versus-Rebalancing (LVR)

This loss is quantifiable, proportional to price volatility, and persists even with fees. It's the main reason LPs often lose money despite earning trading fees.

ZeroSwap's question

Can the AMM infer the true price from its own trade history and update dynamically — without any oracle?

KEY CONCEPTS

6 terms you need to know

📖

Bid / Ask / Spread

Ask = price to buy. Bid = price to sell. Spread = Ask − Bid. A tighter spread = more efficient price.

🎯

Informed vs Noise Trader

Informed traders know the true price and arbitrage. Noise traders are random. ZeroSwap balances them so net loss = 0.

🔒

Hidden / External Price

The true price on Binance. The AMM never sees it directly — it must infer it from who buys vs sells.

🎲

\(\alpha\) (informedness)

Probability an arriving trader is informed. Higher \(\alpha\) = more arbitrage. In practice, the AMM doesn't know \(\alpha\).

📈

\(\sigma\) (volatility / jump prob.)

Probability the hidden price jumps at each step. Higher \(\sigma\) = harder to track. Both \(\alpha\) and \(\sigma\) are unknown in the real world.

🤔

POMDP

Partially Observable Markov Decision Process: a decision problem where the true state matters, but the agent sees only clues. Here the hidden price is the state and trades are the clues.

🎰
THE MODEL

Glosten-Milgrom adapted for DeFi

A 1985 model of market microstructure — adapted to the blockchain setting. Three components work together:

Hidden external price (Random Walk)
$$ p_{t+1} = \begin{cases} p_t + 1 & \text{with prob. } \frac{\sigma}{2} \\ p_t - 1 & \text{with prob. } \frac{\sigma}{2} \\ p_t & \text{with prob. } 1 - \sigma \end{cases} $$
ZeroSwap Objective (Expected Loss = 0)
$$ \text{ask}_t = \mathbb{E}[\, p_t \mid \mathcal{H}_t,\, d_t = \text{BUY} \,] $$ $$ \text{bid}_t = \mathbb{E}[\, p_t \mid \mathcal{H}_t,\, d_t = \text{SELL} \,] $$
\(p_t\)Hidden external price at time \(t\).
\(\sigma\)Probability that the hidden price jumps.
\(\mathcal{H}_t\)History of all past quotes and observed trades up to time \(t\).
\(d_t\)The trader's action: buy, sell, or no trade.
askPrice at which a trader buys from the pool.
bidPrice at which a trader sells to the pool.

When ask and bid equal these conditional expectations, profits from noise traders exactly cancel losses to arbitrageurs. Expected profit/loss = exactly zero. Hence: ZeroSwap.

📐
ALGORITHM 1 — BAYESIAN

When \(\alpha\) and \(\sigma\) are known

Maintain a probability distribution (belief) over all possible hidden prices. Update it with Bayes' Rule after every single trade.

1

Keep a belief \( b(p) \)

A probability distribution over all possible external prices. Tracks the AMM's uncertainty about the true price.

2

Simulate "what if" posteriors

Before the trade: compute what belief would look like if next trade is BUY, and if it's SELL.

3

Solve fixed-point for ask & bid

The quote affects whether traders buy or sell, and that buy/sell signal affects the quote. Start with a guess, update the belief, update the quote, and repeat until the values stabilize.

4

Update belief & diffuse for jump

After trade is observed, update belief. Then slightly blur for potential price jump (controlled by \(\sigma\)).

Result

Gold-standard benchmark. Spread → 0 exponentially fast. Monetary loss ≈ 0%. Theorems 1 and 2 are the paper authors' proof that the Bayesian method converges under their assumptions.

🤖
ALGORITHM 2 — MODEL-FREE

When \(\alpha\) and \(\sigma\) are unknown

Use reinforcement learning. No model needed — just watch trade imbalance (recent buys minus sells) as a signal that the price is off.

Reward Function (Maximised by Agent)
$$ r_t = -n_t^2 - \mu (\text{ask}_t - \text{bid}_t)^2 $$

Where \( n_t \) is the trade imbalance over window \( H \), and \( \mu \) is a spread penalty.

⚖️

Term 1: Balance trades

Reward balanced buy/sell flow — indirect signal that ask/bid bracket the true price.

📏

Term 2: Penalise wide spread

Prevents the trivial exploit of setting ask = \(\infty\), bid = \(-\infty\) to balance trades at zero competition.

Trade imbalanceLower mid-priceDo nothingRaise mid-price
−1: more sells0.80.2−0.4
0: balanced0.10.90.1
+1: more buys−0.30.20.8

These are Q-scores, not probabilities: they may be negative and do not need to sum to 1. The highest score in each row is the preferred action.

The agent learns a Q-table \( Q(\text{imbalance}, \text{action}) \) where actions are \(\pm\)adjustments to mid-price and spread. Standard Q-learning update, run online with no training phase.

Theorem 3

The optimal RL policy's squared deviation from true price is at most a constant multiple of the Bayesian policy's. Spread → 0 exponentially fast in the provable case.

WHY CHANGE ANYTHING?

The original learner saw too little, and the test mixed learning with scoring

1️⃣

One-number state

The last 21 trades were added into one number. The order of buys and sells disappeared.

📦

No inventory input

Inventory is the tokens the market maker is holding or owes after trades. A large position is risky because a price move can turn it into a large loss.

🧪

Unclear evaluation

The agent was still learning while results were measured, so training noise affected the score.

What we kept

The paper's market simulator and its nine small choices for moving the quoted center price and widening or narrowing the spread.

What we changed

A richer observable state, a separate frozen test phase, and explicit inventory safety. We used paper-length paths, but a smaller 3×3 alpha/sigma grid and fewer training episodes because of compute limits.

Frozen test phase

“Frozen” means the model stops learning. We then score the finished policy on new market paths it did not train on.

WHAT WE TRIED FIRST

Three tempting ideas — three important lessons

Using 100 trades of history performed worse than using 21.
What these failures taught us

We needed a short useful state, a balanced reward, and a rule that directly prevents inventory from growing without limit.

1. More history

H=100 made learning harder and performed worse than H=21.

2. Big spread

A wide ask-minus-bid gap gives traders a worse price or stops them trading. It can make profit look better while making the market less useful.

3. Inventory blowup

The model kept accumulating a position instead of closing it. The rare worst result, −1956.86, came from this runaway position.

OUR NEW MODEL

RC-AS-DDQN, translated into normal language

Goal: choose one of the same nine quote actions, using better information and less overconfidence.

RCRisk-Constrained: hard rules limit dangerous actions.
ASAvellaneda-Stoikov: market-making guidance shifts quotes to reduce inventory.
DDQNDueling Double Deep Q-Network: a neural Q-learning model designed to reduce overconfidence.

Pattern finder

A neural network reads the last 10 trades and finds useful short patterns.

Current situation

It also sees spread, inventory, recent direction, trading pace, and the previous quote move.

Double DQN

One network chooses an action; a second checks its score, reducing overconfidence.

Dueling network

It learns how good the situation is separately from which action is best.

Prioritized replay

It practices more often on surprising mistakes stored from earlier trades.

AS guidance

Inventory-sensitive guidance gently shifts quotes toward reducing the position.

RC means risk-constrained

The learner is not free to take every action. Safety rules can block actions that widen risk. The hidden external price, α, and σ are still not shown to the model.

THE REWARD FUNCTION

The exact score used by our final model

Implemented in dqn_reward_history_experiment.py
$$ \begin{aligned} \widetilde r_t &= \operatorname{clip}_{[-10,10]}(r_t),\\ r_t &= \operatorname{clip}_{[-5,5]}\!\left(\frac{\pi_t}{10}\right) - \left(\frac{n_t}{H}\right)^2 - \left[0.05\frac{s_t}{8}+0.5\left(\frac{(s_t-8)_+}{20}\right)^2\right]\\ &\quad - \left[0.25\left(\frac{|I_t|}{I_s}\right)^2 + \mathbf{1}_{|I_t|>I_h}\left(\frac{|I_t|-I_h}{I_s}\right)^2 + 2\mathbf{1}_{t=T}\left(\frac{|I_t|}{I_s}\right)^2\right]\\ &\quad - 0.05\left(|\Delta m_t|+|\Delta s_t|\right). \end{aligned} $$

Final one-sided run: \(H=10\), \(I_s=1\), and \(I_h=20\). The DQN learns from clipped reward \(\widetilde r_t\); the experiment logs the unclipped total \(r_t\).

1. Trade imbalance \(n_t\)

Sum of the last \(H\) trade signs: buy \(=+1\), sell \(=-1\), no trade \(=0\). The squared normalized sum is subtracted.

2. Spread \(s_t\)

\(s_t=\text{ask}_t-\text{bid}_t\). Every unit of spread costs points; spread above the target \(8\) receives an extra squared cost.

3. Inventory \(I_t\)

The held token position. Cost grows with \(I_t^2\), grows again beyond \(I_h\), and receives an extra terminal cost at the final step \(T\).

4. Quote movement

\(m_t\) is the quote midpoint. The reward subtracts \(0.05\) times the absolute midpoint move plus the absolute spread move.

5. Trading result \(\pi_t\)

Buy: \(\pi_t=\text{ask}_t-p_t^{ext}\). Sell: \(\pi_t=p_t^{ext}-\text{bid}_t\). No trade: \(0\). It is divided by \(10\), then clipped.

What the outer clip does

The complete sum is limited to \([-10,10]\) before entering replay memory, preventing rare extreme rewards from dominating DQN updates.

Important correction: training uses an oracle

The policy state does not contain \(p_t^{ext}\), but the trading-result term uses that hidden external price during training. The trained policy can run without it; this implementation's training reward cannot.

THE SAFETY CHANGE

One-sided quoting removes the dangerous trade

Simple rule: if we already hold inventory, we hide the quote that could make that inventory bigger. Here \(I\) means inventory.

I = 0

No position

Show both bid and ask. The next trade may open a one-unit position.

I > 0

Holding tokens

Show only the ask, which allows selling. Hide the bid, which could buy even more.

I < 0

Owing tokens

Show only the bid, which allows buying back. Hide the ask, which could increase the short position.

Why the reward was not enough

The inventory cost could ask the model to reduce risk, but a random trader could still hit the dangerous quote.

Why this rule works

The dangerous quote is absent. With one-unit trades, absolute inventory cannot grow above 1.

Reading the three cases

\(I=0\) means no position, \(I>0\) means the pool holds tokens, and \(I<0\) means it owes tokens. The model still chooses prices; the rule decides which side traders can use.

The improvement

Higher simulated LP result and no inventory blowups. The paired gain was +2.6420 per step, with a 95% bootstrap confidence interval of [2.2625, 3.0514].

The price we paid

The spread was slightly wider, and one side was hidden on 56.9% of steps. Liquidity — the ability to trade either direction at a competitive price — was lower.

Test size

30 training episodes × 20,000 steps, three α values, three σ values, three seeds, and five new test paths. This is a 3×3 subset, not the paper's full 6×10 grid.
